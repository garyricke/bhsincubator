What is INCubator@BHS?

The INCubator Entrepreneurship program at Batavia High School is a flagship Career-Technical Education (CTE) initiative that bridges the traditional divide between classroom instruction and the practical demands of the global marketplace.

Part of the nationally recognized Uncharted Learning network of approximately 90 schools, INCubator@BHS is centered on "Purpose-Driven Learning" — the idea that deeper cognitive engagement occurs when students are passionate about the problems they are solving.

The program is specifically tailored for sophomores, juniors, and seniors, functioning as a high-stakes elective that also satisfies the state-mandated Consumer Education requirement for graduation. This means it's a core component of a student's academic progression — not a fringe extracurricular activity.

“The INCubator@BHS program offers BHS students an authentic entrepreneurship experience. Students are able to develop, test, and modify their own product or service under the mentoring of experienced industry professionals.”
— Brian Gamlin, Director

What Makes It Different

  • Students create real companies — not simulations or theoretical exercises
  • An eight-module Lean Startup curriculum updated annually by Uncharted Learning with input from industry professionals
  • Community volunteers serve as mentors, coaches, and business resources
  • Culminates in a "Shark Tank" style Pitch Night at the Batavia Fine Arts Centre with real seed funding awards
  • Managed financially through the Batavia Foundation for Educational Excellence (BFEE), a non-profit established in 1985

The INCubator Program & Uncharted Learning

Batavia High School's adoption of this model was heavily influenced by the success of the inaugural INCubatoredu program at Barrington High School in 2013. By observing the impact of real-world learning and community-school partnerships, BHS leadership recognized the potential to fill critical gaps in traditional vocational training.

Part of a National Network

INCubator@BHS is a member of a national network of approximately 90 schools utilizing the Uncharted Learning curriculum. Uncharted Learning, a not-for-profit educational organization, provides comprehensive curriculum, teacher training, and ongoing support services. The curriculum is "evergreen" — updated annually with input from industry professionals to ensure it reflects current market trends and technological advancements.

The Eight-Unit Lean Startup Journey

The core of the program is a two-semester sequence that leads student teams through the entire lifecycle of a startup using the Lean Startup process:

  • Unit 1 — Ideation: Competitive analysis, value propositions, identifying market pain points
  • Unit 2 — Customer Discovery: Primary research to understand and validate the target market
  • Unit 3 — Customer Connections: Positioning, marketing channels, customer relationship management
  • Unit 4 — Financial Modeling: Costs, pricing, contribution margin, break-even analysis
  • Unit 5 — MVP Construction: Building a testable Minimum Viable Product
  • Unit 6 — Validation: Rigorous experimentation; teams pivot or persevere based on data
  • Unit 7 — Promotion & Sales: Market entry and acquiring first paying customers
  • Unit 8 — Pitch Prep: Synthesizing the year into a professional investor presentation

Year Two: ACCELeratoredu

For students whose businesses gained traction, the ACCELeratoredu second-year course transitions from a startup simulation into a real-life accelerator — focusing on company formation, legal entities, customer acquisition, and production processes. This demonstrates that INCubator is not a one-year novelty but a sustainable entrepreneurial support system.

The Community-Driven Instructional Model

A distinctive feature of the BHS INCubator program is its departure from the siloed, single-teacher classroom. While the classroom teacher provides continuity and manages administrative aspects, a significant portion of the instruction is delivered by community volunteers. This creates a bridge between the school and the local economy.

Three Tiers of Volunteer Engagement

The program utilizes a three-tiered system for community involvement, allowing local professionals to engage at a level that fits their expertise and availability:

  • Mentors: The most deeply involved — assigned to one specific team for the entire academic year. They provide weekly feedback and encouragement, meeting with students during class or via video calls to guide them through the Lean Startup process. The mentor-student relationship is central to the program's success.
  • Coaches: Subject matter experts who enter the classroom for a limited duration (one to five days) to provide intensive instruction on a specific area — such as business law, marketing, or finance. They collaborate with the classroom teacher to ensure real-world context aligns with academic requirements.
  • Business Resources: As-needed volunteers who serve as judges for preliminary pitch competitions, host on-site student visits at their places of business, or participate in the BHS career fair.

This volunteer structure is not merely a cost-saving measure; it is a pedagogical strategy designed to foster "lifelong learning" and community collaboration. By involving local entrepreneurs, students are held to professional standards and receive feedback grounded in the current realities of the Batavia business landscape.

“The energy in the classroom is contagious — students learning from one another in a way that is the ultimate goal for educators.”
— Karen Roberts, Buffalo Grove High School, INCubatoredu member

Past Student Ventures & Awards

The culminating "Shark Tank" style Pitch Night at the Batavia Fine Arts Centre is where student teams present their finalized business concepts to a panel of industry experts, competing for actual seed funding. These awards represent a significant transfer of community-raised capital directly into the hands of student entrepreneurs.

Award-Winning Ventures

  • Hyra Parking ($2,000): A website and mobile app solving high school parking shortages — students could rent spots to peers, with the company taking a portion of the sale. Demonstrated sophisticated understanding of scalability.
  • West Exit ($1,800): An American-made clothing company modeled after brands like Supreme. Generated over $3,500 in revenue before Pitch Night, leveraging platforms like Reddit for sales.
  • The Sunshine Seat ($1,700): A car seat equipped with a built-in thermometer and smartphone app to alert parents if a child was left in a hot car — exemplifying "purpose-driven learning" by addressing a serious safety issue.
  • GeoFluence ($1,400): A social media advertising company connecting small businesses with influencers. Their door-to-door strategy demonstrated the "customer connection" skills taught in Unit 3.
  • Osa Security ($1,000): An app to prevent sexual assault on college campuses, allowing users to group up with friends and share locations — a "friends helping friends" model of security.

Beyond Pitch Night

The Batavia Chamber of Commerce provides "Inspire Scholarships" to graduating seniors who demonstrate entrepreneurial savvy. Recipients such as Lukas Giesen, who founded the power washing business GTS Solutions, exemplify how INCubator skills translate into actual business ownership — he established a viable business before even graduating high school.

Unit 1: Ideation

The journey begins with problem discovery. Students conduct competitive analysis, develop a value proposition, and identify real pain points in the market. They brainstorm potential solutions and begin evaluating which ideas have the most promise.

This phase teaches students that great businesses start by deeply understanding the people they serve — not by falling in love with a solution too early. The emphasis is on finding problems worth solving.

Unit 2: Customer Discovery

Teams engage in primary research to understand their target market. This involves identifying potential customers, conducting interviews, and — most importantly — validating that the identified problem actually exists for real people.

Students learn to set aside their assumptions and listen. If the data shows the problem isn't significant, they pivot early rather than investing time in something nobody needs.

Unit 3: Customer Connections

This unit focuses on positioning, marketing channels, and customer relationship management. Students determine how to reach their audience effectively — whether through social media, partnerships, events, or direct outreach.

A great example: the student venture GeoFluence used door-to-door outreach to connect local businesses with social media influencers, directly applying the "customer connection" skills from this unit.

Unit 4: Financial Modeling

Students learn foundational finance and accounting principles. They estimate costs, set pricing, calculate contribution margins, determine break-even points, and build financial models to determine the viability of their ventures.

Understanding these variables is essential — when teams reach Pitch Night, they must be able to defend their financial projections to a panel of expert judges who evaluate the business not just as a school project, but as a potential investment.

Unit 5: MVP Construction

This is where ideas become tangible. Teams develop a Minimum Viable Product (MVP) — a testable version of their product or service with just enough features to satisfy early customers and generate learning.

Fundraising proceeds directly support this phase by providing students with the resources to build real prototypes — reducing the personal financial barrier to entrepreneurship.

Unit 6: Validation

Rigorous experimentation and testing of all elements of the business plan. Students collect real data from their MVP and must decide whether to "pivot" (change direction) or "persevere" (double down) based on what they learn.

This is the heart of the Lean Startup methodology — and often the most transformative learning experience, as students discover that failure is not an endpoint but a data point.

Unit 7: Promotion & Sales

Teams plan for market entry, developing detailed strategies for taking their product to market and acquiring their first paying customers. This is where business plans meet reality.

Some teams, like West Exit, generated over $3,500 in real revenue before Pitch Night — showcasing their ability to execute a marketing plan and leverage platforms like Reddit and social media for sales.

Unit 8: Pitch Prep & Pitch Night

The culmination of the course. Students synthesize their entire year of work into a professional presentation for potential investors. They learn to craft compelling narratives, distill complex information, and present with confidence.

The "Shark Tank" style Pitch Night at the Batavia Fine Arts Centre is the public face of the INCubator program. Student teams present their finalized business concepts to a panel of industry experts, competing for actual funding to turn their plans into reality.

The event is described as "inspiring and exciting for all who attend," as it showcases the tangible results of the students' year-long journey and the community's investment.
